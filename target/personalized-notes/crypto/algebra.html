Number Theory

Euler's Totient Function and Modulo Multiplicative Group

Modulo Multiplicative Group: Zn∗​:={a∈Zn​:gcd(a,n)=1} is the set of elements in Zn​ that has a modular inverse under modulo n.

Euler's Totient Function: ϕ(n):=∣Zn∗​∣ (i.e. the number of elements in Zn​ that has a modular inverse).

Multiplicative Order and Primitive Root and Index

Multiplicative Order: For any a∈Zn∗​, the smallest positive integer k that satisfies ak≡1 (mod n) is the multiplicative order. Denoted as ordn​(a).

Primitive Root: Positive interger g that satisfies ordn​(g)=ϕ(n). Primitive roots have the property gϕ(n)≡1 (mod n).

Index (Discrete Logrithm): Exists an unique logg​(a):=x, such that a≡gx (mod n) and gcd(a,n)=1. It is bounded by 0≤logg​(a)<ϕ(n).

  • logg​(ab)≡logg​(a)+logg​(b) (mod ϕ(n))
  • logg​(am)≡mlogg​(a) (mod ϕ(n))
  • logh​(a)≡logh​(g)logg​(a) (mod ϕ(n))

Discrete Logrithm Problem: There does not exists an efficient alogrithm that can quickly compute the index.

Group

(S,⋅) is a group G consists of a non-empty set S and a binary operation ⋅ on any 2 elements of S, with the following axioms being satisfied:

  • Closed: ∀a,b∈G,a⋅b∈G
  • Associative: ∀a,b,c∈G,a⋅b⋅c=(a⋅b)⋅c=a⋅(b⋅c)
  • Unique Identity: ∃e∈G,∀a∈G,a=e⋅a=a⋅e
  • Unique Inverse: ∀a∈G,∃a−1∈G,e=a⋅a−1=a−1⋅a

Subgroup: H:=(U,⋅) is a subgroup of G iff U⊊S and H forms a group.

Abelian Group: a group where ⋅ is commutative for elements in S (e.g., (Z,+)).

Coset

For any a∈G,

  1. aH:={a⋅h:h∈H} is the "left coset of H in G with the representative a"

  2. Ha:={h⋅a:h∈H} is the "right coset of H in G with the representative a"

  3. [a]H​:=aH is the "coset of H in G with the representative a" iff aH=Ha

Coset divides G into subsets based on the relationship between elements in H and any b∈G. All elements of G belongs to some coset, so G=⋃i=1n​[ai​]H​.

a≡b (mod H) iff a and b have the same relative relationship with H (i.e., [a]H​=[b]H​).

For example, let G=Z,and H=3Z={0,±3,±6,⋯}, all elements b in the congruence class (coset) [1]3Z​ all have the same relationship of b≡1 (mod 3).

Operation on Cosets: [a]H​⋅[b]H​:=[a⋅b]H​

Lagrange's Theorem: For any finite group G with its subgroup H, the cardinality of H divides the cardinality of G (i.e., ∣H∣ ∣ ∣G∣). Proof: All cosets in G have the same cardinality as H (the ordinary coset), since there exists a bijection mapping between any cosets [a]H​ and [b]H​; hence, ∣G∣=∑i=1n​∣[ai​]H​∣=∑i=1n​∣H∣=n∣H∣.

Normal Subgroup: N is a normal subgroup iff ∀a∈G,aN=Na

Quotient Group: G/N:={[a]N​:a∈G} (e.g., Z/nZ={[0],[1],...,[n−1]}=Zn​)

Group Homomorphism and Isomorphism

Homomorphism: For groups (G,⋅), (G′,×), if exists functions f:G→G′ such that ∀a,b∈G,f(a⋅b)=f(a)×f(b), then these two groups are said to be homomorphism.

Homomorphic Image: Im(f)=f(G):={f(a):a∈G}. Does not necessarily be the entire G′ (i.e. f may be non-surjective).

Homomorphic Kernal: Ker(f):={a∈G:f(a)=e′}, where e′ is the identity in G′. Does not necessarily be {e} (i.e. f may be non-injective).

Isomorphism: Homomorphism but with the constrain that f is bijective (i.e. f−1 exists). Denoted as G≅G′.

Isomorphic groups are essentially the same group, and all properties are commonly shared.

Fundamental Homomorphism Theorem (FHT): For any homomorphism of group G with a function f, there always exists an isomorphism relationship, G/Ker(f)≅Im(f).

Cyclic Group

⟨g⟩:={gn:n∈Z} is a cyclic group with a generator of g iff ⟨g⟩ is a group (e.g., mZ=<m> is a cyclic group).

Order of Element: For any group G, a∈G, if n is the minimum positive integer such that an=e, then n is the order of element a. This is similar to the multiplicative order for Zn∗​.

  • ∀m∈Z,n∣m⟺am=e.
  • If the order of a finite cyclic group ⟨g⟩=n, then the order of g=n, and g=g2=⋯=gn=g0.
  • Any order n finite cyclic group, if d∣n, then there exists an unique subgroup with order d.
  • For any k∈Z, the order of the element gk is gcd(n,k)n​.
  • Cyclic group with order n has ϕ(n) distinct generators.
  • ⟨g⟩≅Zn​

For integers m=1,2,4,pk,2pk, where p is an odd prime and k is a positive integer, there exists a primitive root g∈Zm∗​. In addition, Zm∗​=⟨g⟩ forms a finite cyclic group, so primitive roots are generators.

Ring

(R,+,⋅) is a ring when:

  • (R,+) is an Abelian group
  • (R,⋅) is closed: a,b∈R⟹a⋅b∈R
  • (R,⋅) is associative: a⋅b⋅c=a⋅(b⋅c)=(a⋅b)⋅c
  • Distributative law holds: ∀a,b,c∈R,a⋅(b+c)=a⋅b+a⋅c and (b+c)⋅a=b⋅a+c⋅a

Additionally, more restrictions can be placed:

  • Ring with Identity: contains an multiplicative identity(i.e. ∃e∈R,∀a∈R,e⋅a=a⋅e=e)
  • Commutative Ring: operation ⋅ is commutative(i.e. ∀a,b∈R,a⋅b=b⋅a)
  • Division Ring: (R∖{θ},⋅) forms a group

Zero Element(θ): An element θ∈R such that ∀a∈R,a⋅θ=θ⋅a=θ. This is also the additive identity (proof: a⋅θ=θ⟺a⋅b=a⋅b+a⋅θ=a⋅(b+θ)).

Identity(e): The multiplicative identity. For any ring with ∣R∣>1, θ=e (proof by contradiction: e=θ⟹∀a∈R,a=e⋅a=θ⋅a=θ).

Zero Divisor: Non zero elements a,b∈R (a=θ,b=θ), such that a⋅b=θ (e.g. 2,5∈Z10​,2⋅5≡0 (mod 10)).

Integral Domain: a commutative ring with identity that there is no zero divisors and ∣R∣>1.

Characteristic

Char R:=m, where m is the smallest positive integer such that ∀a∈R,θ=∑i=1m​a. If such m does not exists, then m=0.

This is similar to the additive order of a given a∈R, except that the characteristic of a ring is for ALL a∈R.

Ideal

(R,+,⋅) is a ring, for some I⊆R, if

  • ∅=I
  • (I,+) is a subgroup of (R,+)
  • I satisfies any of:
    1. ∀r∈R,∀a∈I⟹r⋅a∈I
    2. ∀r∈R,∀a∈I⟹a⋅r∈I

, then I is called the "left ideal of R" if (1) is satisfied, the "right ideal of R" if (2) is satisfied, or simply "(two-sided) ideal of R" if both conditions are met.

Principal Ideal

(aR,Ra):=(a⋅r:r∈R,r⋅a:r∈R) for some a∈R

If aR=Ra, then (a):=aR is the principal ideal of R generated by a.

Prime Ideal

If I⊊R and ∀a,b∈R,a⋅b∈I⟹a∈I∨b∈I, then I is the prime ideal of R (this is similar to p∣ab⟹p∣a∨p∣b in prime factorization).

Field

(F,+,⋅) is a field when:

  • (R,+) is an Abelian group
  • (R∖{θ},⋅) is an Abelian group
  • Distributative law holds: ∀a,b,c∈R,a⋅(b+c)=a⋅b+a⋅c and (b+c)⋅a=b⋅a+c⋅a

All fields are integral domains, but only finite integral domains are fields.
